        <p><strong>Teaching Interests</strong></p>
  <p><p><span>I teach courses in psychology and neuroscience. Because my research focuses on learning and memory and applications to psychological disorders, I teach a variety of classes that speak to these topics. </span></p>
<p><span>Courses I teach in the Psychology Department include Introductory Psychology, Biological Psychology, Learning &amp; Behavior, and Memory &amp; Amnesia. My Neuroscience courses include Brain &amp; Behavior with Lab, Neuroscience of Anxiety, and the CUE in Neuroscience.</span></p>
<p><span><strong>Office Hours:&nbsp;<a href="https://calendly.com/gotthard/30min?month=2022-04" target="_blank">By appointment - click here to schedule a time.</a></strong></span></p></p><hr/>


<p><strong>Research, Scholarship or Creative/Artistic Interests</strong></p><p><p><span>Our work examines how memories are created (consolidated) and modified (reconsolidated) in three organisms<strong><span style="font-weight: 400;">â</span></strong>slime mold (Physarum polycephalum), rats and humans. Our work with Physarum polycephalum<strong><span style="font-weight: 400;">â</span></strong>a single-celled organism that has no brain (!)<strong><span style="font-weight: 400;">â</span></strong>manipulates reconsolidation processes using simple habituation tasks and herbal compounds as amnesic agents (e.g., Valerian root). </span></p>
<p><span>Researchers in our rat lab use appetitively-motivated tasks where rats are trained to dig for buried sweet cereal rewards. We employ pharmacological agents (e.g., protein synthesis inhibitors and beta-adrenergic receptor antagonists) to disrupt (re)consolidation in our rat model. </span></p>
<p><span>Researchers in our human memory lab employ a variety of appetitive tasks that measure not only declarative (conscious) memory (i.e., episodic and semantic memory), but also nondeclarative (less-conscious) memory for the task (e.g., physiological responses). </span></p>
<p><span>The ultimate goal of our lab is to apply our work to aspects of treatment for memory-based forms of human psychopathology (e.g., depression and anxiety).</span></p>
