<h1><span style="color: #1822cd;"><a id="background"></a>Personal Background</span></h1>
<blockquote>
<p>I am originally from Media, Pennsylvania, about half an hour outside of Philadelphia. I received my B.S. with honors in Biological Sciences from Stanford University. Afterwards, I worked as the Life Sciences Intern at The Exploratorium, a science museum in San Francisco. I moved to Ithaca, New York where I worked as a laboratory technician at Cornell University, working first on induced resistance in tomatoes and later on defense in marine invertebrates, especially echinoderm larvae. I entered graduate school at Cornell University and received my Ph.D. in January 2002. My thesis investigated the behavioral ecology of feeding in a marine snail. Most of my research was performed subtidally (underwater, using SCUBA equipment) at the Friday Harbor Laboratories (FHL) in the San Juan Islands, near Seattle, Washington. &nbsp;I still often perform research at FHL during the summer, and have brought a number of Muhlenberg students out there to do research with me.&nbsp; For information about the Friday Harbor Laboratories, <a href="http://depts.washington.edu/fhl/">click here</a>.</p>
<p>After receiving my doctorate (Ph.D.), I taught for one year in the Biology Department at The College of Wooster and I also (on my breaks) worked as an Undersea Specialist, guiding ecotours in Baja California and southeast Alaska with Lindblad Expeditions.&nbsp; These two regions of the world are amazing.&nbsp; Ask me more about them if you are looking for an awesome place to visit and learn a lot about some unusual ecology and ecosystems!&nbsp; I joined Muhlenberg College‚s Biology Department in 2003.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong><span style="color: #1822cd; font-size: medium;"><a id="research"></a>Research Interests</span></strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>I study the ecology of invertebrates, with a particular focus on symbiotic interactions and feeding and defense in marine invertebrates. I use an understanding of the present-day ecology of organisms to shed light on evolutionary questions.&nbsp;&nbsp; My summer research often occurs at sites near the Friday Harbor Laboratories (FHL), on San Juan Island, Washington State, and I have brought Muhlenberg students out to FHL to perform research with me.&nbsp; For one major set of my projects, I&nbsp;am examining <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/biology/ErikaIyengarSeaTable.JPG" alt="Biology" style="  padding : 3;   float : right;" />epibiotic snails that live on other organisms.&nbsp;&nbsp;How species-specific are these symbiotic interactions, why choose one host over another, and what sorts of benefits and costs are incurred by the epibionts and hosts?&nbsp; Much of my recent work has involved the snail <em>Crepidula adunca</em>, which mostly specializes on the top snail <em>Calliostoma ligatum</em> in the San Juan Islands.&nbsp; I am also fascinated by suspension-feeding snails (<em>Crepidula</em> spp. Feed in this manner).&nbsp; These snails collect food from the water column, usually by trapping food particles on their gill.&nbsp; Water needs to travel over the gill anyway so that the snail can obtain oxygen, and the food particles in non-suspension feeding snails are rejected as waste.&nbsp; Why not eat those scrumptious morsels?&nbsp; This is exactly how most bivalves (clams, oysters, scallops, also in the Phylum Mollusca) feed.&nbsp; Yet comparatively few aquatic snails feed in this way, even though this feeding mode is thought to have evolved multiple times.&nbsp; Why?&nbsp; No one knows‚but I enjoy contemplating this as I study suspension feeding snails (such a <em>Crepidula</em> spp. and <em>Trichotropis</em> spp.).<br><br>On-campus, projects in my lab also involve defense strategies of fresh water (stream and pond) insects and snails. &nbsp;I am particularly interested in inducible defenses: defenses that are deployed only when the prey receives a specific cue from a predator.&nbsp; Otherwise, the prey remains undefended.&nbsp; Such inducible defenses might be behavioral, but I am more interested in structural, morphological changes that occur, especially when a prey is faced with cues from various potential predators that forage in different ways.&nbsp; No one individual can do everything, so how does a prey prioritize which defense to deploy?</p>
<p>If you are a student interested in the behavior or ecology of aquatic invertebrates, stop by and let's discuss some of your ideas, interests and possible future programs you might want to investigate.</p>
