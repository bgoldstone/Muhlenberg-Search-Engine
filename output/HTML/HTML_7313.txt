<h2>‚Door Opening‚ Experiences</h2>



Students in Professor Gretchen Gotthard‚s Lab perform research, present at conferences and co-author articles in academic journals.

<p style="color:#4D4D4D;font-size:small;">
By:



  Ava Duskic &rsquo;23  



<span style="text-size:small;">	&nbsp;Monday, March 29, 2021  09:00 AM</span></p>

<!-- FULL WIDTH Image -->
<img src="https://www.muhlenberg.edu//media/contentassets/images/newscenter/Rebecca Shear Neuroscience Gotthard.jpg" style="width:100%;" alt="News Image"><br/><span style="color:#4D4D4D;font-size:small;font-style:italic;">From left, Desiree Bsales ‚20, Alison Bashford ‚20, Jessica-Ann Golbitz ‚19 and Rebecca Shear ‚21 in Professor Gretchen Gotthard‚s Lab in 2019.</span><br/><br/>


<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When Rebecca Shear <span>‚</span>21 started college, she had a plan.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">After attending Muhlenberg‚s <a href="/academics/braincamp/">Brain Camp</a> the summer before her senior year of high school and building a connection with the faculty members there, Shear knew her passion was to pursue a degree in neuroscience </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">and</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> work in a neuroscience research lab with those professors. Happily, reality exceeded her expectations.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Shear has worked in the research lab of Gretchen Gotthard, associate professor of <a href="/academics/psychology/">psychology</a> &amp; <a href="/academics/neuroscience/">neuroscience</a>, since her first year on campus. Along with other student researchers, she has been</span><a href="https://www.muhlenberg.edu/news/2018/hownewtechnologymayhelphealoldtraumas.html"><span style="font-weight: 400;"> conducting experiments that look at memory disruption and whether the emotional aspects of a traumatic memory can be weakened</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">‚Before we believed that if someone has PTSD and they experienced their trauma 20 years ago, the idea was sort of stuck,‚ Gotthard says. ‚Now we know we can remind them of the memory and do different things to change or weaken the memory.‚</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> One of the research projects focuses on usi</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">ng virtual reality to create a memory and then disrupts that memory consolidation process, with the goal of </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">helping PTSD patients in the future.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p>In a second project, students in Gotthard's rat lab worked to determine the parameters for memory reconsolidation (i.e., when a long-term memory is recalled and reactivated). The research suggests people with very old traumatic memories may be helped by reconsolidation techniques as easily as those with newer traumatic memories.</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="/media/contentassets/images/newscenter/Paris-Eiffel-Tower 1-460x784.jpg" alt="" style="   margin : 8px;  float : left;" />In March 2019, Shear, along with five other women student researchers in Gotthard‚s neuroscience lab, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Alison Bashford <span>‚</span>20, Desiree Bsales <span>‚</span>20, Victoria Castillo <span>‚</span>19, Jessica-Ann Golbitz <span>‚</span>19 and Hannah Gura <span>‚</span>20,</span> <a href="https://www.muhlenberg.edu/news/2019/studentspresentatparisconference.html" target="_blank"><span style="font-weight: 400;">presented both research projects </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">at the International Conference for Psychological Science in Paris.</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Later that year, the women, along with Levelle Arnold <span>‚</span>20, Sabrina Barhoum <span>‚</span>21, Jake Dengler <span>‚</span>20, Sara Flash <span>‚</span>20 and Kevan Shah <span>‚</span>22, attended the <a href="http://gretchengotthard.bergbuilds.domains/research/conference-presentations/" target="_blank">Society for Neuroscience conference in Chicago</a>, presenting the same experiments to a new audience.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">‚I knew I was going to be able to do quality research with Professor Gotthard,‚ said Shear, who is a </span><a href="https://muhlenbergsports.com/news/2020/3/16/postcards-from-abroad-rebecca-shear.aspx"><span style="font-weight: 400;">member of the College's volleyball team</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and currently mulling over several Ph.D. neuroscience program offers for the fall. ‚But I never expected to go to Paris or Chicago and be able to present as an undergraduate at these very competitive conferences that people I look up to scientifically and academically are consistently attending.‚</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Gotthard <em>(back row, left, with the student team in Paris</em>) understands how important research opportunities are for undergraduates.</span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> ‚</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;">I was fortunate to do undergraduate and post-baccalaureate research that really opened doors for me,‚ she says. ‚Being a first-generation college student who knew very little about the process of getting into graduate school and being successful in graduate school, the relationships I formed with my research mentors were invaluable. My motivation for working with student researchers is to provide the same ‚door-opening‚ experiences for them.‚&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">However, the opportunities didn‚t end there. The rat lab research continued and after years of hard work, Bashford, Bsales, Golbitz and Shear were listed as Gotthard‚s co-authors when the findings were&nbsp;recently&nbsp;published in the journal </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Learning and Motivation</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;">. As an undergraduate, Shear says this feat gave her an appreciation for the art of writing scientific papers.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A second paper was published in the same journal,</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> with</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Gura listed as Gotthard‚s co-author, along with Allison Davidson, associate professor of <a href="/academics/mathcs/">statistics</a></span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The team<span>&nbsp;</span>worked to determine what kind of prediction errors were needed to break down a memory, curating an experiment where human participants tried to remember a video they watched after being subjected to different prediction error conditions.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">‚Former researchers in my lab have been successful in a wide variety of areas including doctoral programs in neuroscience, clinical psychology, and neuropsychology; medicine and veterinary medicine; physical and occupational therapy; law school and public health programs,‚ says Gotthard. ‚I like to think that some of the success my former research assistants have had is due in part to their research experiences at Muhlenberg.‚</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
