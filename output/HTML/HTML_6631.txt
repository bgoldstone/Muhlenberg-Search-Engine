<h2>How Does a Bachelorâs or Masterâs Degree Increase Your Earning Potential</h2>

<img src="/media/contentassets/images/gce/blogs/Earning potential with a degree.png" style="float:left; margin:8px 8px 8px 0" alt="News Image">



<p style="color:#4D4D4D;font-size:small;">




  



<span style="text-size:small;">	&nbsp;Monday, August 9, 2021  01:00 PM</span></p>

<!-- FULL WIDTH Image -->



<p>When asked why theyâre seeking a college degree, adult learners offer many reasons. Some want to pursue their passion. Some want to earn a specific job title. And some want to forge a new career path. But at some point, all adult learners ask themselves the same question: Can I really increase my earning potential with a degree?</p>
<p>To help adult students find the answer, we dove into the research. And no matter how you look at the data, achieving a bachelorâs or masterâs degree will indeed increase the average studentâs earning potential. By how much? Letâs dive into the numbers and find out.</p>
<p><strong>Weekly earnings for college graduates</strong></p>
<p>The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) tracks the earning potential of a degree by median weekly earnings for workers ages 25 and older. Their most recent findings from 2019, which BLS <a href="https://www.bls.gov/careeroutlook/2020/data-on-display/education-pays.htm">published in May 2020</a>, show that adults with bachelorâs degrees will earn <strong>$502 more per week</strong> on average than people with only a high school diploma. Adults with masterâs degrees will earn <strong>nearly twice as much per week</strong> on average than those with a high school diploma.</p>

<p><strong>Yearly earnings for college graduates</strong></p>
<p>Now, what happens if you take those weekly figures, extrapolate them out over an entire calendar year and figure in taxes? The College Board did the math for us. They release an annual Education Pays report. The most recent one, released in 2019 and based on 2018 data, show that adults with bachelorâs degrees will earn $24,900 more after taxes per year than adults with only a high school education. Adults with masterâs degrees will earn $39,700 more per year than those with a high school diploma.</p>

According to Education Pays, 43% of advanced degree holders aged 35 to 44 earned $100,000 or more in 2018. Thatâs compared to 28% of those with bachelorâs degrees, and 5% of people with a high school diploma.</p>

<p>A studentâs major will impact his or her potential career earnings. The Education Pays report broke down median earnings of early career and mid-career college graduates who have a bachelorâs degree. For purposes of the chart below, early-career means ages 22 to 27, while mid-career means ages 35-45.</p>

<p>Not surprisingly, many of the majors listed above are in STEM (science, technology, engineering, mathematics)-related fields. Trailing right behind the top 10 majors are nursing, general business, communications, all three of which have mid-career median earnings of $70,000. On average, people with bachelorâs degrees will earn $40,000 per year in their early career and $68,000 per year by mid-career.</p>
<p><strong>How earning a degree impacts your career possibilities</strong></p>
<p>Clearly, the data show how bachelorâs and masterâs degree attainment will increase your earnings potential. Experts advise adults that the best time to start their education is now. According to a <a href="https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/college-interrupted%3A-the-case-for-going-back-to-school-2021-04-15">July 2020 report from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York</a>, delaying enrollment for one year can cost a yearâs worth of wages over your lifetime.</p>
<p>The benefits of adult education go far beyond earnings. BLS statistics show that <strong>unemployment rates for people with masterâs (2%) and bachelorâs (2.2%) degrees are lower</strong> than the unemployment rate of people with only a high school diploma (3.7%).&nbsp;</p>
<p>And while attaining a bachelorâs or masterâs degree as an adult doesnât guarantee youâll get a pay increase, it does improve your negotiating power. In general, employers view workers with bachelorâs or masterâs degrees as having a greater level of subject matter knowledge.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Employers also know that college graduates, including many from the <a href="https://www.muhlenberg.edu/continuingstudies/">Muhlenberg College School of Continuing Studies</a>, work on solving real-world business problems during their courses of study. College graduates offer skills like discipline, goal setting and collaboration that employers desire.</p>
