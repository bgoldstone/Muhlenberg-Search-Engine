<p>First-year students contemplating majoring in accounting are encouraged to consult with Trevor Knox, Accounting Coordinator, as early as possible during their first year.</p>
<p>Due to the sequencing of courses, please do not place undeclared students in any course without checking the catalog for prerequisites (e.g., Marketing, Management, and Entrepreneurial Studies).</p>

<p>In rare instances an incoming student chooses to take just one science class in the first year.&nbsp; Any student making this choice should be aware that he or she may not have time to complete all of the preparatory courses for the MCAT in spring of the junior year.</p>

<p>Traditionally, students admitted to law school have come from almost every academic discipline.&nbsp; Students may choose to major in subjects that are considered to be traditional preparation for law school, such as history, English, philosophy, political science, economics or business, or they may focus their undergraduate studies in areas as diverse as art, music, science and mathematics, computer science, or education.&nbsp; Whatever the major, students are encouraged to pursue an area of study that interests and challenges them, while taking advantage of opportunities to develop critical thinking, research and writing skills, and oral communication.&nbsp; Students are also recommended to seek significant experiences in and outside the classroom which are devoted to improving fairness, the quality of justice, and a commitment to public service.</p>

<p><strong>Humanities (HU)</strong></p>
<p>Courses that provide a broad understanding of history, including the various social, political, intellectual, ad cultural factors that have influenced the development of society in the United States.</p>
<p><strong>Social Sciences (SL)</strong></p>
<p>Courses that provide an unerstanding of political thought and of the contemporary American political system, as well as a basic understanding of human behavior and social interaction.</p>
<p><strong>Natural Sciences and Mathematics (SC)</strong></p>
<p>Courses that develop basic quantitative and scientific problem solving skills to investigate natural phenomena.</p>
<p><strong>Human Difference and Global Engagement (DE)</strong></p>
<p>Courses developing an understanding of diverse cultures within and beyond the United States, of international institutions and issues, of world events, and of the increasing interdepence of the nations and communities within our world.</p>

<p><strong>NOTES:</strong>&nbsp; If schedule permits because of opting out of requirements, students might be encouraged to take courses in the natural sciences (e.g., biology or neuroscience), in other social sciences (e.g., sociology or anthropology), or in Humanities courses addressing the human condition.&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p style="padding-left: 30px;">PSY 212: Learning and Behavior<br> PSY 220: Social Psychology<br> PSY 230: Child Development</p>

<p>Since the prerequisite for Psych Stats is either Math 119 or Cal I, students who place into Cal II or higher would be considered to have satisfied the math prerequisite for Psych Stats.&nbsp; There are currently seats open for the course, and we believe that the students will have sufficient mathematical sophistication to handle the quantitative parts of the course.&nbsp; HOWEVER, Psych Stats also has a major writing component, in which students are introduced to APA writing style and application. Thus, students considering taking Psych Stats in their first semester should be asked about their level of comfort with the amount and type of writing that they will be required to do. If the student isn't confident in their writing ability, they should consider delaying Psych Stats until they already have some college-level writing under their belts.</p>
<p>Students scoring a 4 or 5 on the AP Psych Test need not take Intro to Psych, depending on their golas and circumstances. They would be better served taking a 200-level psychology course from the list in the advising manual, or to focus on the general academic curriculum and take a psychology course second semester.</p>

<p>The WST Program at Muhlenberg offers a six-course minor that students can combine with any major.&nbsp; Recent WST graduates have majored in biology, psychology, mathematics, media and communication, philosophy, sociology, history, English, religion studies, and theatre.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>They will need to take WST 202</strong>. This core WST class teaches students the international history of Women‚s and Gender Studies and the latest thinking in these areas: feminism, sexuality studies, masculinity studies, and queer studies.&nbsp; (This course is full for Fall 2021)</p>
<p>First year students curious about exploring Women‚s &amp; Gender Studies (which encompasses topics of sexuality studies, intersectional analysis, cultural studies, queer theory and more) <strong>should try to take one of the classes on the WST Electives list during their first year.</strong></p>
<p><strong>Minor Requirements</strong></p>
<p>To complete the Women‚s and Gender Studies minor, students must take WST 202, Topics in Women‚s and Gender Studies (or an equivalent course designated by the Director) and five additional courses from the list of approved classes. A WST internship, study abroad, and independent study classes can also count toward the minor.&nbsp; In addition to the Women‚s &amp; Gender Studies courses (listed below), special topics courses cross-listed with WST are available on a semester-by-semester basis.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>Courses from WST Electives list that DO NOT have </em></strong><strong><em>prerequisites and DO have seats for incoming first-year students:</em></strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">AAS 383: Global Black Lit (may be full)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">AFS 101: Intro to Africana Studies</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">ATH 250: Anthro of Gender &amp; Sexuality (may be full)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">ENG 287: Spc Top: Trans &amp; Gender Variant Literature</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">HST 281: Special Topic: Eliza Hamilton‚s America (may be full)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">NCS 389: Special Topic: Neurobiological of Sex Differences (requires permission of instructor, but interested students should email <a href="mailto:leahwilson@muhlenberg.edu">leahwilson@muhlenberg.edu</a> for more information or for permission to register)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">PHL 227 Philosophy of Feminism (may be full)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">Students with previous gender studies experience might try PSC 383: Spc Top: Critical Race Feminist Theory, which recommend but does not require past courses.</p>
<p><em>*Note for any students who express an interest in a Self-Designed Major in Gender &amp; Sexuality Studies: The students should contact Dr. Coppa. There are several students currently completing a Self-Designed Major in Gender and Sexuality Studies through the Women‚s and Gender Studies program with advising by WST faculty.</em></p>
