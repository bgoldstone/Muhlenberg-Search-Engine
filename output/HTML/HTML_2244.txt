  <h1 style="text-align: center;"><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Italian Studies Prize</span></h1>
<h5 style="text-align: center;"><strong><a href="/academics/llc/languages/italian/">Home</a> <strong><strong><strong><strong><strong><strong><strong>|</strong></strong></strong></strong></strong></strong></strong>&nbsp;<strong><a href="/academics/llc/majorsandminors/italianstudiesminor/">Minor</a>&nbsp;|&nbsp;<strong><a href="/academics/llc/faculty/italianfaculty/">Faculty</a>&nbsp;|</strong>&nbsp;<a href="/academics/llc/courses/italiancourses/">Courses</a> <strong>| <a href="/academics/llc/onlineresources/italianresources/">Resources</a> <strong><strong><strong><strong><strong><strong><strong>|</strong></strong></strong></strong></strong></strong></strong> Club | <a href="/academics/llc/languages/italian/events/">Events</a></strong></strong></strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;"><br></span></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/llc/italian/corona_2_1.jpg" alt="" style="   margin : 0px auto; display: block; " /></p>
<hr>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In 1341, the great poet&nbsp;<strong>Francesco Petrarca</strong>&nbsp;became the first man to be crowned Poet Laureate in Rome since classical antiquity. With his revival of this ancient ceremony ‚&nbsp;the placement of a laurel crown (<em>la corona d'alloro</em>)&nbsp;on the head of a poet by the Roman Senate ‚&nbsp;Petrarca sought to raise the dignity of poets to that which they had enjoyed in ancient Rome. He began his acceptance speech by quoting the poet Virgil ‚&nbsp;<em>sed me Parnasi deserta per ardua dolcis / raptat amor</em>&nbsp;("but over the lonely heights of Parnassus, a sweet love captivates me,"&nbsp;<em>Georgics,</em>&nbsp;Book<em>&nbsp;</em>3) ‚&nbsp;expressing the difficulty of the solitary labor required by academic pursuits coupled with the gratifying exhilaration of intellectual successes.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The&nbsp;<strong>Corona d'Alloro Prize in Italian Studies<em>&nbsp;</em></strong>is awarded each year to the student who best demonstrates intellectual excellence in Italian Studies at Muhlenberg. The prize was inaugurated in 2015 thanks to a financial gift from a very generous anonymous donor interested in promoting Italian Studies at Muhlenberg College.</p>
<h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<hr>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3><span style="font-size: 1.17em;">2021</span></h3>
<p><strong><strong><img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/llc/italian/Alex Mast-230x276.jpg" alt="" style="  padding : 5px;   float : left;" /></strong></strong></p>
<p><strong><strong>Alexander Mast</strong> <strong>'21</strong> </strong>is the winner of&nbsp;of the 2021 Corona d'Alloro Prize in Italian Studies.&nbsp; Alex is a Music Major and Italian Studies Minor. Arriving at Muhlenberg as a sophomore transfer with two semesters of French on his transcript, Alex decided to shift his gaze south to the sunny climes of <em>il bel paese: </em>a fateful decision.&nbsp; Immediately following his first Italian class, ears still ringing with mellifluous Italian vocabulary, lyrical sentences, and dreaming of stones filled with stories amongst the streets of Italy‚s most beautiful cities, Alex was confident that his migration to Italy was the right decision.&nbsp; Before he knew it, Alex was committing the Italian Studies Minor to compliment his major in Music. Italian Studies minorled Alex to study abroad&nbsp; in Florence, where his stories became a part of those stones he heard about in class back in Allentown. Senior year, he successfully launched a project as president of the Italian Club entitled <em>La Passeggiata</em>, a weekly newsletter meant to engage with the club‚s audience and provide a weekly dose of the magic that Italian students experience at Muhlenberg every day in class.</p>
<p>&nbsp;The following are Alex's reflections on studying Italian at Muhlenberg.</p>
<p><em>We sat upon gritted leather, upholstered decades ago, within the echoey, boxy living room of our Florentine apartment, ceilings near twelve feet tall... </em><a href="/media/contentassets/pdf/academics/llc/italian/Mast Prize Essay.pdf">Alex Mast Prize Essay</a></p>

<h3><span style="font-size: 1.17em;">2019</span></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">‚<strong>‚</strong><img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/llc/italian/Norman_headshot_250.jpg" alt="" style="  padding : 5px;   float : left;" /><strong>Rachel Norman '19</strong> is the winner of the 2019 Corona d'Alloro Prize in Italian Studies.&nbsp; Rachel is a Theatre Major and Italian Studies Minor, who hadn't planned on pursuing Italian when she first arrived on campus. However, after taking the History of Italian Theatre course she was hooked and delved right in, becoming one of the most committed and enthusiastic students of Italian on campus. Among her accomplishments was her successful tenure as Italian Club president in spring 2019, and her co-development, with Prof. Leisawitz, of a new integrated-learning course <em>‚ </em>Translating &amp; Performing Italian Drama <em>‚ </em>which entailed a collaboration with the Italian Playwrights Project (NYC) and resulted in an original, student-devised translation and performance of an original piece of Italian drama. In addition to her accomplishments in Italian, Rachel is an actress and <a href="http://www.thewimn.com/rachel-norman-releases-debut-ep-resist-inspired-family-relationships-being-female/" target="_blank">singer-songwriter</a>. The following are Rachel's reflections on studying Italian at Muhlenberg.</p>
