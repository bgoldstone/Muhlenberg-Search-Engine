  <h2>Kate Richmond, Ph.D.</h2>
<p><img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/psychology/faculty/richmond.jpg" alt="Psychology" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" /><br><strong>Associate Professor of Psychology</strong><br><strong>Director of Women &amp; Gender Studies</strong><br><a href="mailto:krichmond@muhlenberg.edu" target="_blank">krichmond@muhlenberg.edu</a><br> 484-664-3423<br><br></p>
<p><strong>Please join the department in congratulating Dr. Richmond on these prestigious national awards.&nbsp;<a href="https://www.muhlenberg.edu/news/2020/providingmentorshipandinspirationtoallwomen.html">Read more about all three awards here.</a></strong></p>
<ul>
<li><span>2020 Emerging Leader for Women in Psychology Award</span></li>
<li><span><span>2020 Florence Denmark Award</span></span></li>
<li><span><span><span>2020 Distinguished Publication Award for&nbsp;Kate's&nbsp;<a href="https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393667134"><em>Psychology of Women</em></a> textbook.</span></span></span></li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><em><strong>Teaching Interests:</strong></em></p>
<p>I teach courses that bring together my passion for psychology, women &amp; gender studies, mental health, and social justice. These include <em>Psychology of Women, Multicultural Psychology, Inside/Out Prison Exchange, Introduction to Women &amp; Gender Studies, Research Methods, Abnormal Psychology, and Clinical Case Studies. </em></p>
<p>My classes are student-centered. I am energized by the <em>process</em> of learning. Most people believe that the professor is the one who holds knowledge. Yet, knowledge is not something to give; it is something that is in flux and in need of critical engagement. In this way, even when a course has the same title or covers the same content, each class takes on its own dynamics. After the semester ends, I am never quite the same, and my hope is that students are transformed too.</p>
<p>I teach because it is a high stakes undertaking. Our individual and collective futures depend on intellectual communities. As learners, we try to question and resist detrimental messages that can be absorbed unnoticed. Without the skills to evaluate, challenge, and reinvent, there is no opportunity for empowerment. Far worse, there is great potential for harm, since so much of the dominant discourse increases fear and anxiety. Together, we work to move away from indoctrination and toward the risky experience of becoming. Through this process, we develop compassion for each other, since we repeatedly uncover that living with freedom and responsibility is complicated and difficult. Ultimately, we know that it is not simply enough to engage in the classroom; we must also continually and impatiently work toward the liberation of each other.</p>
<p>I&nbsp;have been honored with the <em>Paul C. Empie Memorial Award for Excellence in Teaching</em>, the <em>First Year Advising Award,&nbsp;</em>and the <em>Henry Award for Faculty Member of the Year.</em></p>
<hr>
<p><em><strong>Research Interests</strong></em></p>
<p>I research in the areas of multicultural psychology, gender ideology, men and masculinity, transgender resiliency, feminist therapy, and trauma. In my research, I explore how social identity factors, such as your gender or race, influence mental health outcomes. I also explore how certain gendered experiences, like becoming a father or experiencing menstruation, can influence your relationships with others.</p>
<p>My undergraduate textbook, <em>Psychology of Women &amp; Gender, </em>was published by W. W. Norton in November 2018. The Association of Women in Psychology&nbsp;honored this work&nbsp;with the 2020 Distinguished Publication Award.</p>
