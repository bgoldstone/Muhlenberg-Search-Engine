<p><strong>March 11, 2022<br></strong></p>
<p><strong>Linda McGuire</strong>&nbsp;, Truman Koehler Professor of Mathematics, published The EPaDel Community (w/ X. Catepillan) in Count Me In: Community &amp; Belonging in Mathematics (D. <p><strong>March 3, 2022</strong></p>
<p><strong>Spring 2022 Colloquium Series:</strong><br>Murong Xu, Department of Mathematics, University of Scranton</p>
<p>New DigitalWatermarking Alorithm and its Application.</p>
<p>Digital watermarking is the process of hiding digital information in a carrier signal. The feature codes (such as hash value or message authentication code) of an object to be authenticated are self-embedded as a watermark, and then feature extracted in the same way will be compared with the watermark retrieved to determine if there are any changes. In such way, the ownership of the copyright of such signal could be identified. Digital watermarking is a very important technique on cryptography study.</p>
<p>For a grayscale digital image, the value of each pixel is a single sample representing only an amount of light, that is, it carries only intensity information. The pixel value is ranged from 0 to 255, in which Black is 0 and White is 255. So every grayscale digital image watermark can be converted into a matrix and embedded to the digital image. A good watermarking should not only make the hidden watermark invisible to human eyes, but also be robust enough to survive most signal distortions.</p>
<p>In this talk, a new image digital watermarking algorithm will be introduced and simulation results will be presented as well.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>February 10, 2022</strong></p>
<strong>Spring 2022 Colloquium Series:</strong><br>John Kriter, Senior Technical Architecture at Accenture (a multinational information technology company)
<p>Synopsis: For the last 10 years, John has focused his energy on helping organizations around the world implement technology solutions to solve problems. Joining Accenture as a Computer Science Major, he has applied his knowledge and skills working with &gt; 25 different organizations across &gt; 10 countries. As a Technical Architect, John has received numerous certifications from organizations like AWS, Microsoft, Google, Ali, Tencent, and CNCF.&nbsp; When not toying with some new piece of technology, John is an avid 3D printer &amp; motorcycle enthusiast.</p>
<p>During this session, we will explore some of the pivotal experiences this career has brought. We will look at some tips on how to learn and grow within the technology space, life working with large brands in corporate environments, and some of the in-demand skillsets in the current job market.</p>

<p style="padding-left: 30px;">This talk begins with a practical home construction question encountered by my father in his volunteer work at Habitat for Humanity.&nbsp; After considering this and a few related geometry problems, we will discuss the connection between these problems and the goals and aims of the Mathematics Teacher‚s Circle Network.</p>

<p><strong>February 26 - Math/CS Presentation<br></strong></p>
<p><strong>Linda McGuire</strong>, Muhlenberg College: <strong>Other</strong><em><strong> Hidden Figures</strong></em><strong> in Mathematics </strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">National data trends indicate a critical need to increase representation in the mathematical sciences. By sharing a series of mathematical stories and probing a few mathematical topics, we will consider how issues of identity, visibility, and belonging, influence who does mathematics. We will also discuss ways that we, as a community, might better promote inclusion in the practice of mathematics, statistics, and computer science.</p>

<p><strong>January 31 - Math/CS Colloquium Series</strong></p>
<p><strong>Elizabeth Eisenhauer</strong> is a PhD candidate in the Statistics Department at Penn State. She grew up in Cape May County, NJ and earned a BA in mathematics from The College of New Jersey. She currently works with Dr. Ephraim Hanks at Penn State on animal movement modeling.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>A Lattice and Random Intermediate Point Sampling Design for Animal Movement</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">Animal movement studies have become ubiquitous in animal ecology for estimation of space use and analysis of movement behavior. In these studies, animal movement data are primarily collected at regular time intervals. We propose an irregular sampling design which could lead to greater efficiency and information gain in animal movement studies. Our novel sampling design, called lattice and random intermediate point (LARI), combines samples at regular and random time intervals. We compare the LARI sampling design to regular sampling designs in an example with common black carpenter ant location data, an example with guppy location data, and a simulation study of movement with a point of attraction. We modify a general stochastic differential equation model to allow for irregular time intervals and use this framework to compare sampling designs. When parameters are estimated reasonably well, regular sampling results in greater precision and accuracy in prediction of missing data. However, in each of the data and simulation examples explored in this paper, LARI sampling results in more accurate and precise parameter estimation, and thus better prediction of missing data as well. This result suggests that researchers might gain greater insight into underlying animal movement processes by choosing LARI sampling over regular sampling.</p>

<p style="padding-left: 30px; text-align: left;"><strong>How Odd are Odd Perfect Numbers?</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px; text-align: left;">Greek mathematicians defined a whole number to be perfect if it is the sum of its proper divisors, where ‚proper‚ means the divisor is smaller than the number itself.&nbsp;&nbsp; An example is&nbsp; 6 ,&nbsp; because the sum of its proper divisors is&nbsp; 1 + 2 + 3 = 6 .&nbsp;&nbsp; Since classical times, 51 perfect numbers have been found ‚¶ and every one of them is even.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px; text-align: left;">That raises an obvious question: are there any odd perfect numbers?&nbsp; To date, no one knows the answer.&nbsp; It is one of the true mysteries of mathematics.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px; text-align: left;">In this talk, we trace the history of perfect numbers.&nbsp; This will lead us to an 1888 argument in which J. J. Sylvester proved that, if an odd perfect number does exist, then it must have at least three different prime factors.&nbsp;&nbsp; Although this property is far from self-evident, Sylvester‚s elegant proof requires nothing more sophisticated than geometric series.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px; text-align: left;">So, we‚ll see a bit of history and a bit of mathematics as we dabble in the challenging realm of number theory.</p>

After retiring from Muhlenberg College (emeritus, 2014), he has held visiting positions at Harvard, Princeton, Penn, Cornell, and Bryn Mawr, where he and wife Penny currently are Research Associates in Mathematics.</p>
