<h1 class="gateway-toolbar-portfolio gateway-toolbar-item"><span class="acalog-icons-visible">Media &amp; Communication Structure Courses</span></h1>
<div class="gateway-toolbar-portfolio gateway-toolbar-item"><span class="acalog-icons-visible">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div class="gateway-toolbar-portfolio gateway-toolbar-item"><span class="acalog-icons-visible"><a href="http://www.muhlenberg.edu/main/academics/mediacom/requirementscourses/">Return to Requirements &amp; Courses</a></span></div>
<div class="gateway-toolbar-portfolio gateway-toolbar-item"><span class="acalog-icons-visible">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM&nbsp;<span class="acalog-highlight-search-1">205</span>&nbsp;- Asian/American Media</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Students will examine diverse Asian media representations in cinema, television, and new media in relationship to the geopolitical and community history of Asian diaspora in the U.S. &nbsp;It will critically interrogate stereotypical images of Asian/Asian-American identities, culture, religions, and politics as well as representations that challenge and contest such stereotypes. &nbsp;In doing so, the course will locate the politics of Asian media representation within a broader historical, political, cultural, and aesthetic context that includes issues of cross-cultural appropriation, globalization, immigration, nationalism and citizenship, race, ethnicity, gender, class, and sexuality.<span>&nbsp;</span><br></span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span><em>Meets general academic requirements SL and DE.</em>&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>
</div>
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM&nbsp;<span class="acalog-highlight-search-1">210</span>&nbsp;- Media Law</strong></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Introduces the philosophy, history, development, and current interpretations of U.S. media law; explores constitutional rights, laws, precedents, and public concerns which guide U.S. media, the public, the courts, regulatory agencies, and policymakers.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 244,&nbsp;245 - Media &amp; Social Movements</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines the interrelationship between mass media and twentieth century social movements in the United States.&nbsp; How have actors within social movements used mass media to raise awareness, mobilize, and/or demand redress?&nbsp; How have various mass media portrayed those movements, actors, and events?&nbsp; Using an historical approach, we will explore how context - technological change, political, social, and economic climates - deeply influence how mass media and social movements interact. &nbsp;Primary attention will be given to social movements during the age of the Cold War (1945-1990), including the Civil Rights/Black Power, the New Left, the New Right, Feminist, and Gay Rights Movements.&nbsp; Students will be challenged to consider local examples of present-day social change advocacy in relation to media use and representation.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><span></span><span></span><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Meets general academic requirement HU (and W when offered as 245).</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em>&nbsp;</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 312&nbsp;- Media Industries</strong></p>
</div>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">1 course unit</p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Considers the forces (legal, political, economic, historical, and cultural) that shape what we watch on television, read in books, or hear on the radio.&nbsp; Explores a wide range of print and electronic media industries as well as developing media like the Internet.&nbsp; Economic and critical analysis is used to examine both the institutional forces and individualized decisions that ultimately shape the content and format of mass media messages. &nbsp;Selected topics include media conglomeration, target marketing, media integration and digital television, and globalization of media markets.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 314&nbsp;- Audience Analysis</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines the concept of audiences from a variety of qualitative and quantitative research perspectives: as ‚victims,‚ users, subcultures, and market commodities. &nbsp;Television ratings, public opinion polls, and other strategies for measuring audience feedback are analyzed and assessed.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><span>&nbsp;</span></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 316</strong>&nbsp;<strong>- Propaganda &amp; Promotional Cultures</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines the historical development, social roles, communicative techniques, and media of propaganda.&nbsp; Thematic emphasis varies from semester to semester with case studies drawn from wartime propaganda, political campaigns, advertising, and public relations.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><span><span></span><span></span><em>Meets general academic requirement SL.</em><br></span></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><span><em>&nbsp;</em></span></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 319&nbsp;- Play &amp; Interactive Media</strong></p>
</div>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines videogames as a theoretically contested object and pervasive cultural form.&nbsp;&nbsp; Considering questions of play, pleasure, narrative, computation, genre, art, industry, embodiment, violence, race, gender, and sexuality, students will closely play videogames and critically analyze them in terms of their formal structure and aesthetics as well as their social and ideological contexts.<span>&nbsp;</span></span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 341&nbsp;- Social Media &amp; the Self</strong></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Explores the performance of identity on social networking sites like Facebook and Tumblr, against the backdrop of the history of consumer culture.&nbsp; A core theme is the tension and overlap between ideals of authenticity and self-possession.&nbsp; Other themes include subcultural style, emotional labor in the workplace, and self-help culture.&nbsp; Students explore the online self with the emergence of the internet and into the Facebook era, with an emphasis on changing definitions of public and private, algorithmic memory, gender and sexuality, and the economics of sharing.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>
</div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 344&nbsp;- Documentary Film &amp; Social Justice</strong></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines documentary and other non-fiction based modes of film, video, and digital media production and the assumptions these forms make about truth and authenticity and how they shape our understandings of the world.&nbsp; Both historical and contemporary forms will be considered.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><span></span><span></span><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Meets general academic requirement AR.</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em>&nbsp;</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 346&nbsp;- Exploratory Cinema</strong></p>
</div>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Examines the origin and growth of ‚avant-garde‚ cinema.&nbsp; Traces the history of film and video art from the early 1920s to the present, focusing on its structural evolution, thematic shifts, coexistence with commercial cinema, and its impact on contemporary media.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Meets general academic requirement HU.</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 350 - Children &amp; Communication</strong></p>
</div>
<p><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>This course investigates the meanings of media in children‚s lives. &nbsp;It adopts a cultural historical approach to understanding the role of media in children‚s cognitive, social, and moral development.&nbsp; Looking at children‚s interactions with media artifacts, it considers how childhood is constituted by the languages and images of media and situates these interactions within the broader political economic context constructing the child consumer. &nbsp;Children‚s media studied include television programs, video and computer games, films, books, toys, and the Internet.&nbsp;</span></p>
</div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em>&nbsp;</em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 370&nbsp;- Popular Culture &amp; Communication</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Traces the development of popular forms with emphasis on the ways that social class has structured access, use, and creation of cultural artifacts and practices. &nbsp;Topics explored include both commercial and non-commercial forms of amusements, leisure, and entertainment.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><em><em>Prerequisite(s):</em><span>&nbsp;</span><em><a href="/academics/mediacom/requirementscourses/required/">COM 201 Media &amp; Society</a>.</em></em></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;">&nbsp;</p>
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 372, 373 - Race &amp; Representation</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Explores the social construction of the concept of race and barriers to communication erected by prejudice, discrimination, and marginalization of minority voices.&nbsp; Examines topics in multicultural, cross-cultural, and interpersonal communication as well as analysis of documents, personal narratives, and media images.&nbsp; Primary emphasis is placed upon African American experience in the U.S.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em><em><span></span><span></span><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Meets general academic requirement DE (and W when offered as 373).</em><br></em></em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em><em>&nbsp;</em></em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 374&nbsp;- Gender, Communication, &amp; Culture</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>This course explores how culture establishes, maintains, and cultivates gender through forms of social movements, communication, and institutional structures, particularly commercialized media.&nbsp; Students will examine how youth and adults are socialized to think, talk, and make sense in American culture; the implications of these differences for the construction of gendered identities (e.g., masculinity, femininity, transsexuality), communication, and relationships; and the construction of gender in media, including digital and print advertising, television programs, the Internet, books, magazines, video games, and the cinema.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><em><em><em>Prerequisite(s):</em><span>&nbsp;</span><em><a href="/academics/mediacom/requirementscourses/required/">COM 201 Media &amp; Society</a>.</em></em></em></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em><em><em>&nbsp;</em></em></em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix">
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 378, 379 - Sport, Culture, &amp; Media</strong></p>
</div>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Explores the cultural artifacts, historical developments, and related systems of power that comprise sport media.&nbsp; Students observe, document, and analyze mediated sport and its prominence in our cultural environment. &nbsp;Includes analysis of the conventions of sports journalism (electronic and print) and transformations in those arenas. &nbsp;Emphasizes writing.&nbsp;</span></p>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em><em><em><span></span><span></span><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Prerequisite(s):</em><span>&nbsp;</span><em><a id="tt7007" href="http://catalog.muhlenberg.edu/search_advanced.php?cur_cat_oid=3&amp;search_database=Search&amp;search_db=Search&amp;cpage=1&amp;ecpage=1&amp;ppage=1&amp;spage=1&amp;tpage=1&amp;location=3&amp;filter%5Bkeyword%5D=com+378&amp;filter%5Bexact_match%5D=1#tt7007" target="_blank">COM 201 Media &amp; Society</a>.</em><br><em>&nbsp;</em></em></em></em></div>
<div class="ajaxcourseindentfix"><em><em><em><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;Meets general academic requirement W when offered as 379.</em></em></em></em></div>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 450&nbsp;-&nbsp;Dublin Seminar (Sp 2020)</strong></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>The Dublin Seminar</span><span><span>&nbsp;</span>is offered every spring by the Resident Director of the Media &amp; Communication and Film Studies dedicated study abroad program in the Dublin, Republic of Ireland.&nbsp; The course is taught at Dublin City University in an accelerated format.&nbsp; Each spring has its own focal topic, designated by the faculty member, and may include mobile media, community media, image ethics, media spaces, or contemporary European cinema.<span>&nbsp;</span></span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>COM 470 -<span>&nbsp;</span></strong><span class="s2"><strong>Media</strong></span><span class="s1"><strong>&nbsp;&amp; Communication Honors Seminar</strong></span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">Each year this course will have a different thematic focus which will allow honors and non-honors seniors to engage with faculty and visiting lecturers in challenging dialogues and research experiences, culminating in the production and presentation of an original research project or creative work based on the seminar theme. Provides students with extensive opportunities to work closely with faculty mentors in developing their research project and creative work. Fall only.</p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><strong><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Prerequisite(s):</span></em><span>&nbsp;</span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Permission of the instructor.</span></em></strong></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="color: #7a0109;"><strong>FLM&nbsp;336 -<span>&nbsp;African American Cinema</span></strong></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>1 course unit</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>This course surveys African American filmmaking from the silent ear to the present, along with a few films that represent the broader African Diaspora. In addition, readings put all the films in the context of theoretical discussions concerning what constitutes ‚black,‚ ‚African,‚ or ‚Third Cinema,‚ politically and aesthetically. As the course proceeds chronologically, it briefly demonstrates images of African Americans in mainstream Hollywood films, but focuses primarily on how filmmakers of African descent have sought to respond to mainstream representations and create their own narratives and styles. The emphasis is on narrative films,</span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Attendance at weekly screenings is required.<br style="margin-left: 12px;"><em>Meets general academic requirement DE.</em></span></p>
<p style="margin-left: 12px;">&nbsp;</p>

<p style="margin-left: 12px;"><span>Special Topics Courses are not offered every year, but still count toward the major.&nbsp; Check the Curiculum Checklist or with your advisor to see what special topics are availble.</span></p>
